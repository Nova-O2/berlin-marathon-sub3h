Dear Editor,

We are pleased to submit our manuscript entitled "Pacing Variability, Not Half-Marathon Speed, Distinguishes Sub-3-Hour Success from Failure: A Cluster Analysis of 9,585 Runners at the Berlin Marathon" for consideration in *Sports Medicine*.

This study addresses a long-standing question in endurance sport science: among runners with sufficient aerobic fitness to break 3 hours in the marathon, why do so many fail?
Using a novel quasi-experimental design---the "Golden Window"---we matched 9,585 Berlin Marathon finishers (1999--2025) on half-marathon split time, isolating pacing execution as the sole variable.
Our key findings are:

- Runners who succeeded and those who failed passed the half-marathon within 3 seconds of each other (1:28:05 vs 1:28:08), yet their finish times diverged by over 13 minutes.
- K-Means clustering identified two pacing archetypes: a controlled "Positive Split" pattern (98.3% success) and a "Metabolic Crash" pattern (71.3% failure).
- Pacing coefficient of variation alone predicted failure with AUC = 0.965; a threshold of 5.65% achieved 99% sensitivity.

We believe this work offers three contributions to *Sports Medicine*: (1) a novel matching design that disentangles fitness from pacing; (2) the largest cluster analysis of sub-3-hour marathon runners to date; and (3) a practical, data-driven CV threshold that can be directly integrated into real-time coaching tools and GPS-enabled wearables.
