Dear Editor,

We are pleased to submit our manuscript entitled "Pacing Variability, Not Half-Marathon Speed, Distinguishes Sub-3-Hour Success from Failure: A Cluster Analysis of 9,585 Runners at the Berlin Marathon" for consideration as an Original Research Article in *Sports Medicine*.

**What we found:** Using a novel quasi-experimental "Golden Window" design, we analyzed 9,585 Berlin Marathon finishers (1999–2025) who reached the halfway mark at virtually identical pace but diverged dramatically in outcome. Despite near-identical half-marathon times (1:28:05 vs. 1:28:08), runners who failed to break 3 hours exhibited 3.8 times greater pacing variability. K-Means clustering revealed two archetypes—"Positive Split/Fade" (98.3% success rate) and "Metabolic Crash" (71.3% failure rate). A single metric, the pacing coefficient of variation, predicted failure with AUC = 0.965.

**Why this is important:** While previous studies have linked pacing variability to marathon performance, none has been able to disentangle fitness from strategy. By matching runners on half-marathon capacity, our design isolates pacing execution as the sole variable of interest—demonstrating that for sub-3-hour aspirants, the barrier is strategic, not physiological.

**Why Sports Medicine:** This study addresses a question of broad interest to the endurance sport community—coaches, athletes, and technology developers. The identification of a practical, data-driven CV threshold (5.65%) computable from standard GPS data has immediate translational value for real-time pacing guidance, a topic of growing relevance as wearable technology becomes ubiquitous in recreational running.

The manuscript has not been published elsewhere and is not under consideration by another journal. All authors have read and approved the final version and declare no competing interests.
